Dice Tray

I’m… actually pretty proud of this. This is one of the wood working projects I’ve spent the most time on. I cut strips of cedar and bought foam for the bottom and leather (fake leather) to line the whole thing. I sanded the sides, did my best to line them up, and even put foam feet on the bottom.

The outside corridor can house several sets of dice and even minis, and the center has plenty of room for wreaking mass havoc on players who think the chest in the corner is just a chest.

Paint Box

I fell into a trap and started buying Warhammer 40k figures for a time. While I haven’t played all too much, I had a hobby of painting figures for about a year and amassed a collection of paints, brushes, and other figure materials. This was one of my first complex wood working projects and neatly housed everything I need to fully paint minis.

A fantastic example of engineering oversight is the bolt latch which due to the doors opening sideways, keeps nothing closed.

Scissors Lift

I had aspirations (and future plans) to mount the rubber band turret on top of the scissors lift and have a hidden turret unit. For now, it remains a fun proof of concept. This uses a servo motor and linkage bar to turn the rotations into linear movement of a simple scissors lift mechanism.

The mechanics for this were modeled in Onshape, a free online based 3D modeling software. The servo motor was controlled by an Arduino which could raise and lower the unit at the push of a button. Current maximum load: about 1 Kindle (with case).

Table

I’d been scouring an online market for months telling myself I’d finally buy a table and invest in furniture. However, with a budget of $18, living in a relatively small city, and requirements that it be quite spacious and stout, my options were limited (non-existent). So, I put my recent power tool investment to the task and created my own 4’ x 6’ table for eating, crafting, and gaming.

Wood working is pretty cool. You can go from nothing to exactly what you want and can spend as much or as little time as you want or need. This was built in an afternoon and was everything I needed. A little smoke and mirrors (a tablecloth), and it almost looked like it belonged in a house!

Conch Street Art

Among the many things I learned in my college level intro to Java course was a wonderfully inefficient method of painting. This interactive program uses java.swing and java.awt APIs to draw and color the three homes of Spongebob, Patrick, and Squidward. The user running the program can choose which of the three’s houses to see (or all), as well as the background color.

This project was one of the first times I felt clever while programming, and that was due entirely to the background gradient code. While relatively simple, it produced a nice result… though it was likely a manual implementation of something the API already did.
